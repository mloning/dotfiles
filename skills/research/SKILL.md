# Research

Scope before searching; scale effort to the question; cite only what you actually fetched.

1. **Quiz me first.** Before any searching, ask focused scoping questions to narrow the field — the precise question, the decision it informs, scope/constraints, depth wanted, and what "answered" looks like. Ask one at a time or as a short batch, each with your recommended default. Don't search until the brief is sharp; restate it in the report.
2. **Scale & decompose.** Fact-find → a few searches; comparison → a few angles; open-ended → scout the landscape first, then split into 3–6 distinct sub-questions (different perspectives, not keyword variants). Breadth-first, then drill into specifics.
3. **Prefer primary sources.** Papers, official docs, changelogs, first-party blogs > reputable secondary > SEO listicles (pointers only, never citations). Source quality is the #1 silent failure.
4. **Triangulate & try to falsify.** Require ≥2 independent-origin sources per load-bearing claim; tag each VERIFIED / UNVERIFIED / DISPUTED; actively search for criticism and counter-evidence. Report disagreements — don't average them.
5. **Cite only what you fetched.** URL + date on every claim — never cite from memory (even grounded agents fabricate URLs). Separate fact from opinion.
6. **Synthesize answer-first; know when to stop.** Lead with the answer (BLUF), then evidence, then open questions (each with what would resolve it). Stop when ~2 searches in a row yield nothing new.
